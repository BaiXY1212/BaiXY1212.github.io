Urban Ovens:
How Australian Cities Breathe, Burn, and Cool

On a summer’s day, the streets of Melbourne shimmer while the parks stay surprisingly cool.
What creates these hidden temperature borders within our cities?

This data story takes you on a journey through ten years of satellite records, revealing how urban design quietly shapes the heat we feel — and how nature can help us cool down again.

Heatwaves Turn Up the Dial

Australia is heating up faster than ever.
With extreme heatwaves striking more often, urban surfaces across the nation absorb and radiate unprecedented warmth. This section visualises the surface temperature response of Australia’s seven major capitals — showing how ordinary warmth transforms into severe heat stress, revealing the growing thermal vulnerability of our urban cores when the weather turns extreme.

How to Explore
• Toggle between Normal and Extreme to reveal how heatwaves reshape each city’s surface temperature pattern.
• Click a city on the Dumbbell chart to focus on its local profile — this action also updates all subsequent visuals across the story.
• You can also change the city anytime using the global selector in the top-right corner.

Key Insight
Switching from Normal to Extreme conditions unveils a striking surge in surface heat. The maps make this visible at a glance — cooler tones contract, and hot zones spread outward as the temperature climbs. The Dumbbell chart quantifies this escalation: across every capital, the mean land-surface temperature jumps sharply under heatwave conditions. Yet the extent of this intensification differs from city to city — a clue that deeper structural forces are shaping the heat we feel.

What we see here is the symptom — an abrupt spike in urban heat. But what causes certain parts of the city to heat so much faster than others? The next section digs deeper into the city’s surface itself, analysing how different land-use types and urban forms shape these temperature patterns and drive the intensity of heat build-up.

The Anatomy of a Hot City

The anatomy of heat is written in the city itself.
If heatwaves expose our cities’ thermal vulnerability, this section dissects the structures that create it. Each urban landscape — from dense commercial grids to suburban sprawl — interacts differently with sunlight, vegetation, and materials. By comparing land-use types and their average surface temperatures, we can trace how urban form translates directly into heat.

How to Explore
• The city displayed here follows your selection from the Dumbbell chart or global menu above.
• Hover over a land-use category (on either chart) to see its thermal signature and trigger the linked highlighting.
• In the Sankey Diagram (right), compare the thickness of the lines — they represent the magnitude of each land-use type's thermal impact, revealing exactly how urban structure contributes to the city's heat zones.

Key Insight
The pattern is clear: built-up zones dominate the city’s heat anatomy. Industrial and commercial areas emit the most heat, while vegetated and water surfaces stay comparatively cool — a thermal contrast of more than 10 °C in some cities. This difference shows that urban heat is not random; it is engineered by the density, materials, and spatial layout of our built environment.

Yet these patterns also reveal opportunity. Cities with higher proportions of open or green land — such as Canberra and Darwin — display more balanced thermal profiles, suggesting that urban composition itself can moderate heat build-up.

What if we could harness that balance deliberately?
The next section explores the cooling power of green — showing how vegetation density reshapes surface temperatures and offers a path to urban resilience.

The Cooling Power of Green

If heatwaves reveal our cities’ vulnerability, vegetation shows their capacity to heal.
Having seen how urban form intensifies heat, we now turn to nature — the element that can be designed, planted, and scaled. Unlike water bodies, which are largely fixed, green infrastructure is a controllable force in city planning — a living tool to rebalance urban temperature and restore comfort.

To understand this power, we trace the relationship between NDVI (vegetation density) and LST (land surface temperature) across a decade of satellite records. As greenness rises, temperature falls — a pattern that repeats city after city. Each point on the scatterplot marks a month; each slope tells the same promise: more leaves, less heat.

How to Explore
• The city displayed here follows your selection from the Dumbbell chart or global menu above.
• The left chart visualises the correlation between NDVI (greenness) and LST (surface temperature) for each city.
• The right chart animates this balance through time, showing how drought, development, or re-greening reshape each city’s thermal rhythm.
• Use the Play button or slider to follow how vegetation loss and recovery bend the temperature curve decade by decade.

2014

Key Insight
The data speak softly but clearly — where vegetation thrives, surface heat retreats. Within each city, NDVI and LST move in opposite directions: where vegetation thrives, surface heat retreats. This cooling effect extends beyond parks into surrounding suburbs, proving that urban greenery is not cosmetic, but systemic.

Yearly patterns reinforce this: cities investing in green corridors — like Canberra or Darwin — show milder peaks in surface temperature, while those with dense urban infill face steeper climbs. The thermal rhythm of a city is not destiny — it is design.

Strategic Outlook
Cooling is no longer just a climate goal — it’s a planning choice. When streets are shaded, courtyards greened, and rooftops revived, the city’s microclimate transforms. The lesson across all three chapters is simple but urgent: we built the heat, and we can unbuild it — leaf by leaf, block by block.
